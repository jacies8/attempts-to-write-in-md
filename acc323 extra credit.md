Q1: When you look at a company's deferred tax accounts, what are the most common temporary differences that create deferred tax liabilities and what are the most common ones that create deferred tax assets? What permanent differences come up most often?

A1: For DTLs, the most common temporary differences are tax depreciation and book depreciation. For DTAs, net operating losses are most common. For permanent differences, executive compensation and limitations on meals and entertainment are common.

---

Q2: When a company holds investment securities, what portion of those would you typically expect to see classified as available-for-sale versus held-to-maturity, and what does the decision to hold to maturity actually commit the company to in terms of how it reports those bonds?

A2: Mostly always available for sale because companies don't like held to maturity securities because of the need to mark to market on financial statements.

---

Q3: What happens to a company's earnings per share when it buys back its own stock, and why do executives often prefer buybacks over dividends?

A3: Buybacks increase EPS because there are less shares outstanding. This makes EPS larger and makes the company seem more profitable, which looks good on the executives of the company. However, this is somewhat "old" thinking.

Q4: For a company that grants restricted stock awards to executives, how is the total compensation figured out, and how does it get spread across the financial statements over the vesting period?


A4: Usually, total compensation for restricted stock awards are performance based. The value of the award is fair value at inception (grant date), and it is spread across the earning period. For example, if the vesting period is three years, then the award will be spread out over the three years equally.

Q5: If a company changes an accounting estimate, does it go back and restate prior years, or does it just move forward with the new estimate? 

A5: The company will move forward with the new estimate. Revisions are done prospectively, and there will be a disclouse in the financial statement. Companies will not go back and restate prior year. This will especially take too long if books from many years need to be adjusted. Changes are fixed in the current and future years.

---

Q6: When a company buys back its own shares and holds them as treasury stock, why does that show up as a reduction of equity rather than as an asset on the balance sheet?

A6: Becuase treasury stock is a non earning asset. This means that treasury stock is treated as equity rather than an asset on a balance sheet even though the company owns it.

---

Q7: What is the difference between basic EPS and diluted EPS, and what kinds of securities (like convertible bonds or stock options) could potentially push diluted EPS lower than basic EPS?

A7: Basic EPS is the standard formula calculated with net income, perferred dividends, and shares outstanding. Diluted EPS is calculated with anything that can be possible converted into stock like convertible debt, RSUs, and more. Convertible debt, RSUs, and other stock options could potetionally push diluted EPS lower than basic EPS because net income will be spread out more.

---

Q8: For a company using the equity method to account for an investment, how does the investment account on the balance sheet actually move around over time and what makes it go up and what makes it go down?

A8: For a company using the equity method to account for an investment, increases happen when the investment makes a profit, and decreases happen when dividends are paid.

---

Q9: When a company has a net operating loss on its tax return, what options does it have under current tax law, and how does that loss show up on the GAAP financial statements in the year it occurs?

A9: The main option under the tax law is a carryforward. Companies can carryover its loss indefinitely to offset 80%. The loss shows up as a deferred tax asset in the year it occurs.

---

Q10: If a company declares a small stock dividend instead of a cash dividend, what happens to total shareholders' equity and why does the mix of accounts within equity change even though the total stays the same?

A10: Shareholders equity stays the same, but the mix is different. This is because the stock dividend doesn't involve an outflow of cash like the cash dividend does. The mix changes even though total equity stays the same.
